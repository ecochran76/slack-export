Something that I mentioned to Eric, that I thought I'd put down in here as well for folks to think about, the use of Vitala (SESO) as a plasticizer for PVC, could be looked at. Fully epoxidized vegetable oils (i.e. soybean, linseed, corn, etc.) are some of the most, if not the most common PVC plasticizer. SESO, which is partially epoxidized, would certainly plasticize PVC as well. The question is if it can do it any better. SESO vs ESO is about the same price, so there would need to be a performance advantage. This is something that would need to be tested, but would be relatively simple to do. Surprisingly, a precursory glance does not show much in the literature of epoxy content versus plasticizing effect. If this is of interest, I could ask Andrew to take a look at it. It would take maybe 2 days to get some preliminary data (1 day to compound, and 1 day to tensile test).

I'm not sure how much background information you have, so if I'm telling you things you already know feel free to tell me. I'm hoping that what I mention below triggers a "oh yeah there's a need for this in X application" kind of discussion. I know that formulating new commercial products is something you're keen on and I'll do what I can to help in that regard. ESO and other epoxidized veg oils main uses commercially from what I can find are as plasticizers, reactive diluents for epoxy systems, thiol/acid/alcohol (alcohol is much more poor) scavengers, and as a dispersant for pigments. The big things to keep in mind for SESO/Vitala (if you have a preference on how we refer to it, please let me know), is that it has lower epoxy functionality and it still has some carbon-carbon double bonds remaining. For a lot of those applications, on the surface, that's a bad thing because you want as much of the epoxy content as possible. There are applications (like in hot mix asphalt) where the higher functionality can actually cause problems because of the higher degree of polarity, or causing it to become too cross-linked, too stiff, or too polar upon reaction. Are there applications where the more polar or higher functionality is actually a problem? This would most likely be the case in non-polar waxy type systems, aromatic systems like asphalt or lignin, etc. Are there applications where the residual double bond would actually be useful? An application where you can actually take advantage of some oxidative instability coupled with the epoxy chemistry. Basically you can envision an application where the first step is to react in the SESO through the epoxy and then heat the heck out of it to graft to the double bond. It's going to discolor, but that doesn't matter to every application. And then there's the idea that Baker had. ESO is used as an acid/mercaptan scavenger, we could take advantage of that to impart functionality, change solvating power, etc. Anyway, if at some point you want to really dive into the formulation/development side of things, Baker and I would be happy to participate
